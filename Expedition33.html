Expedition 33

“Cycles, Ritual, and Memory”

Philosophical Concepts

Clair Obscur's: Light-DarkExpedition 33 is a narrative-driven tactical RPG that delves into the mystery of the Expeditions, the Paintress, and the GommageErasure. Within the world of Clair Obscur, the people are faced with death from the Paintress, who each year winds down the clock from 100—marking the oldest age anyone can reach before their Gommage. The annual Gommage shapes the lives and destinies of all, as the threat of erasure looms over every generation. This dread of inevitable death is a central theme that permeates the game, influencing the culture and psyche of its inhabitants.

Players guide the main cast of characters who set out on the 33rd Expedition to fight and kill the Paintress and end the Gommage. Themes of sacrifice, resilience, and the indomitable human spirit emerge as players wrestle with the mortality of their characters and the desperation in their struggle to end the cycle. The game reflects how societies construct rituals to confront the inevitability of violence and death, echoing the ideas of René Girard in Violence and the Sacred.

The cyclical structure of the Expeditions also highlights the relationship between memory, forgetting, and identity. Each Expedition is both a continuation and a repetition of the ones before, suggesting that memory itself becomes a ritual. This theme resonates with the reflections of Paul Ricoeur in Memory, History, Forgetting, who argues that collective memory both preserves and reshapes cultural identity. Similarly, the oppressive inevitability of the Paintress recalls Hannah Arendt’s discussion in The Human Condition, where she emphasizes the tension between mortality, plurality, and the search for meaning.

At its surface, Expedition 33 is a thrilling exploration of how humanity struggles against insurmountable odds. At its philosophical core, however, the game becomes a meditation on ritual, memory, and mortality—an interactive stage where players contemplate the very conditions of existence and the cycles that bind human life.

The Twist

One of the most striking aspects of Expedition 33 is how it challenges the player’s expectations of its story. At first, the game presents itself as a narrative driven by a set goal: to kill the Paintress. With each failed expedition, "For those who come after" is the rallying cry that inspires each individual to never give up for the sake of future expeditioners. [1]. This setup leads us to believe we understand both the trajectory and the eventual outcome. Yet, as the expedition unfolds, the story deliberately subverts these expectations, revealing a twist that reframes the entire journey [2].

After defeating the Paintress, the narrative pivots into a family drama that dives into the raw psychology of loss and grief [3]. The focus narrows onto how individuals process the death of a loved one, revealing fractured relationships, unspoken regrets, and the lingering pain that binds characters together. Instead of providing easy closure, the story examines how grief reshapes bonds between people and how the past continues to haunt the present.

After her defeat, the story reveals that the setting of the story is not a literal world, but a fictional, painted reality created as an escape for the Desenre family to cope with their grief over Verso's death in the real world. [4]. Painted Verso and Maelle’s journey is revealed to take place inside this constructed realm, a fragile refuge born from the family’s inability to confront their grief in the real world. Verso’s death becomes the central wound, the event that both defines the family’s sorrow and locks them into the very cycle they are desperate to escape. By grounding its themes of loss and repetition in both family psychology and a world literally painted from grief, Expedition 33 demonstrates how a narrative can disrupt expectations while reaffirming its deepest ideas, showing that cycles of mourning are not easily broken—but through reflection, they can be understood, confronted, and perhaps transcended [5].
